CONTRACT LAW



Contract law is a set of rules that govern agreements between two or more parties.
A contract is a legally binding agreement that outlines the terms and conditions of a transaction or relationship.

Contract law sets out the following key elements that are necessary for a contract to be enforceable:


  1. Offer: One party makes an offer to another party.

  2. Acceptance: The other party accepts the offer.

  3. Consideration: Both parties must provide something of value in exchange for the agreement.

  4. Intent: Both parties must intend to create a legally binding agreement.

  5. Legality: The subject matter of the contract must be legal.


In simpler terms, contract law is the set of rules that governs how people and businesses make and keep agreements with each other,
and what happens when one party fails to keep their end of the bargain.


TYPES:

There are several types of Contract Law


  1. Express Contracts: An express contract is one in which the terms and conditions of the agreement are explicitly stated in writing or verbally.

  2. Implied Contracts: An implied contract is one in which the terms and conditions of the agreement are not explicitly stated, but can be inferred from the conduct of the parties involved.

  3. Unilateral Contracts: A unilateral contract is one in which one party makes an offer that can only be accepted by the other party performing a specific action.

  4. Bilateral Contracts: A bilateral contract is one in which both parties make promises to each other to perform certain actions.

  5. Executed Contracts: An executed contract is one in which both parties have fulfilled their obligations under the agreement.


OBJECTIVE:

The main objective of contract law is to provide a legal framework that enables parties to enter into and enforce agreements with each other.

Overall, the objective of contract law is to promote trust and confidence in commercial relationships by providing a clear legal framework for parties to enter into and enforce agreements.
